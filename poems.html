⸻ Nine Poems ⸻

"I have walked past armies and forgotten them.
I have never forgotten a lit window."

Begin
I
Poem I

The Waterfall Village

  • Where water finds the stone below
  • and lights inside the windows glow,
  • I stopped and set my staff aside—
  • some places ask you not to stride
  • but simply stand, and let the sound
  • remind you what it means: found.
II
Poem II

Houses in the Trees

  • They did not build upon the earth.
  • They chose the boughs, and found their worth
  • in height that holds, not height that soars—
  • in vine-laced steps and leaf-hung doors.
  • To live among the branches so
  • is to refuse to let things go.
III
Poem III

The Night Canopy

  • When stars are few and teal mist thick,
  • the lanterns do their quiet trick:
  • they do not light the whole—just near.
  • Enough to know that someone's here.
  • A village in the dark still breathes.
  • It simply breathes beneath the leaves.
IV
Poem IV

Morning Over Still Water

  • The pond holds everything it's seen—
  • the tilted roof, the flowering green.
  • I knelt and watched myself below:
  • the same grey robe, a softer glow.
  • What water shows is not a lie.
  • It is the world with less of sky.
V
Poem V

The Curving River

  • It did not ask to be admired.
  • The river simply goes, untired,
  • past cottages with curling eaves
  • and bridges laced with morning leaves.
  • I followed it a little way.
  • Then sat. Some paths are meant to stay.
VI
Poem VI

The Floating Platforms

  • Between the falls, the houses cling
  • to roots that learned a different thing:
  • not downward, outward — upward, wide,
  • with waterfalls on every side.
  • I wondered: does the mist make art,
  • or does the art make mist a start?
VII
Poem VII

The Winding Path

  • No road here runs without a bend.
  • The path insists you cannot end
  • in haste — it curves, it dips, it shows
  • another door each time it goes.
  • A village built on gentle turns
  • has learned what every scholar earns.
VIII
Poem VIII

The Great Tree

  • One tree. And then: a life inside.
  • They asked the oak if it would hide
  • a stair, a window, three small rooms.
  • The oak said yes. Now lamplight blooms
  • from wood that was already old.
  • Some welcomes don't need to be told.
IX
Poem IX

The Crystal Square

  • At the centre, water rises—
  • not from sorrow, not from crises,
  • but because someone long ago
  • believed a village needs a glow
  • at its heart, a gathered place.
  • I stopped here. Rested. Found some grace.
X
Poem X

The Spired Village

  • One tower stood among the rest,
  • not proud — it simply faced the west.
  • The others kept their rooftops low,
  • content to let their lanterns glow.
  • A village needs one thing that's tall:
  • to know it has not lost it all.
XI
Poem XI

The Valley Rope

  • A rope bridge is a kind of faith —
  • it says: I trust what I can't weigh.
  • The stream below runs cold and clear,
  • the houses lean as if to hear.
  • They built across, not just beside.
  • Some villages refuse to hide.
XII
Poem XII

The Curled Eaves

  • When rooftops curl like sleeping ferns,
  • the builder knew what no one learns
  • in stone alone — that shelter bends,
  • that warmth begins where hardness ends.
  • I sat between those curling eaves.
  • The forest held its breath. No leaves.
